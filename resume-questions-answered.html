Crafting the Perfect Resume: Your Top Questions Answered

April 10, 2025 • By Leon

The job search can feel like navigating a maze, and your resume is your map. But what happens when the map-making rules seem confusing? You're not alone! Many job seekers search for answers to the same resume questions every month. "Should I put references on my resume?" "Does it need periods?" "Past tense or present?"

Relax! We've compiled the most common resume questions (based on what people like you are searching for) and provided clear, actionable answers to help you craft a document that gets noticed for the right reasons.

Let's dive in and debunk some myths!

1. The Big Question: Should I Put References On My Resume?

Short Answer: Generally, no. This is one of the most frequently asked questions, and the standard advice has evolved. Decades ago, including references directly on your resume was common practice, but not anymore. Here's why you should leave them off:

  • Valuable Space: Resume real estate is precious, ideally kept to one page (or two for highly experienced professionals). Listing references takes up space better used for highlighting your skills, experience, and achievements.
  • Privacy for Your References: Your contacts haven't necessarily consented to have their information shared widely on resumes sent out en masse. Protect their privacy until it's genuinely needed.
  • Control: You want to give your references a heads-up before a potential employer contacts them. This allows them to prepare and expect the call. If their info is on your resume, they could be contacted unexpectedly at any stage.
  • It's Assumed: Employers know you will provide references if required. They typically ask for them later in the hiring process, often after the first or second interview.

What to do instead:

  • Prepare a separate document listing your references (3-5 professional contacts is standard) with their name, title, company, phone number, and email address. Include a brief note about your relationship (e.g., "Reported directly to Ms. Smith").
  • You can include the phrase "References available upon request" at the bottom of your resume, but even this is becoming dated and is largely optional. Most recruiters assume you have them.
  • Have this separate reference list ready to provide immediately when asked.

2. Tense Talk: Should My Resume Be in Past Tense?

Short Answer: Mostly yes, but use present tense for your current role. Consistency and accuracy are key when deciding if your resume should be written in past tense.

  • Past Roles: Describe all duties and accomplishments for previous jobs using the past tense. (e.g., "Managed," "Developed," "Increased," "Implemented"). These responsibilities and achievements are completed actions.
  • Current Role: Describe your ongoing responsibilities in your current job using the present tense. (e.g., "Manage," "Develop," "Increase," "Implement"). These are tasks you are actively performing. However, use the past tense for specific accomplishments completed within your current role (e.g., "Increased sales by 15% in 2023").

Example:

Current Role:

  • Manage a team of 5 software developers.
  • Develop new features for the company's flagship product.
  • Implemented a new agile workflow in Q1 2024, reducing bug reports by 20%.

Previous Role:

  • Managed project budgets up to $500k.
  • Developed marketing campaigns across multiple platforms.

3. Picture Perfect? Should You Put a Picture On Your Resume?

Short Answer: Generally no, especially in the US, Canada, and the UK. Thinking about whether you should put a picture on your resume? While common in some countries (like Germany) and specific industries (like acting or modeling), adding a photo is generally advised against in many parts of the world for several reasons:

  • Anti-Discrimination Laws: Employers want to avoid any appearance of bias based on age, race, gender, attractiveness, etc. A photo can unintentionally introduce unconscious bias into the screening process.
  • Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS): Many companies use ATS software to scan resumes before a human sees them. Photos can confuse these systems, potentially causing your resume to be parsed incorrectly or even rejected.
  • Professionalism: In many corporate cultures, photos on resumes are seen as unprofessional or distracting.
  • Space: Like references, a photo takes up valuable space.

Exceptions:

  • If you are applying for jobs in countries where photos are the standard convention.
  • If you are in a profession where your appearance is directly relevant (e.g., acting, modeling).
  • If a specific job application explicitly asks for one (though this is rare for standard corporate roles).

Recommendation: When in doubt, leave it out. Focus on presenting your qualifications clearly.

4. Grade Point Average: Should You Put GPA On Your Resume?

Short Answer: It depends, but usually only if you're a recent graduate AND it's impressive. Deciding whether to put your GPA on your resume involves considering a few factors:

Include If:

  • You are a current student or recent graduate (within 1-3 years of graduating).
  • Your GPA is high (generally 3.5 or above on a 4.0 scale).
  • The employer specifically requests it or it's common in your target industry (e.g., finance, consulting, academia for entry-level roles).
  • It was required for academic honors (e.g., summa cum laude).

Exclude If:

  • You have more than a few years of professional experience (your work achievements become far more relevant).
  • Your GPA is average or low (below 3.0-3.5). Don't highlight potentially weaker areas.
  • You have relevant work experience that speaks louder than your grades.

Where to put it: If you include it, place it next to your degree information in the Education section (e.g., "Bachelor of Science in Marketing, XYZ University - GPA: 3.8/4.0").

5. Honesty Policy: Should I Lie On My Resume?

Short Answer: Absolutely NOT. It might seem tempting to stretch the truth, inflate titles, or fabricate skills, but the answer to "Should I lie on my resume?" is a definitive no. It's a terrible idea with serious consequences:

  • You'll Likely Get Caught: Background checks, reference checks, and interviews are designed to verify information. Experienced recruiters can often spot inconsistencies.
  • Immediate Disqualification: If discovered during the hiring process, you'll be removed from consideration.
  • Job Loss: If discovered after you're hired, it's often grounds for immediate termination.
  • Reputation Damage: Your professional reputation can be permanently tarnished, making future job searches difficult.
  • Ethical Concerns: It's simply dishonest.

What to do instead: Be honest. Focus on highlighting your genuine strengths and accomplishments. If you have gaps or weaknesses, be prepared to address them honestly if asked (a cover letter can sometimes help proactively frame these). It's always better to be truthful and find a role that's a genuine fit.

6. Location Matters: Should You Put Your Address On Your Resume?

Short Answer: Your full street address is generally unnecessary and potentially risky. City, State, and ZIP code are usually sufficient. Wondering if your resume should have your address? The tradition of including a full mailing address is fading due to:

  • Privacy Concerns: Sharing your exact location widely isn't ideal in the digital age.
  • Potential Bias: Some worry about location bias (e.g., assumptions based on neighborhood or commute time).
  • Irrelevance for Remote Work: If applying for remote roles, your specific street address is irrelevant.
  • Outdated Practice: Most communication is via email or phone.

What to do instead:

  • Include City, State, and ZIP Code. This gives employers a general idea of your location without oversharing.
  • If you're applying for jobs in a different location and plan to relocate, you can state: "Relocating to [City, State] in [Month, Year]" or address it in your cover letter.
  • Ensure your phone number and professional email address are clearly listed.

7. Punctuation Points: Should My Resume Have Periods? (Especially After Bullet Points?)

Short Answer: Be consistent. Generally, use periods for full sentences but omit them for sentence fragments in bullet points. There isn't one single "right" way regarding whether your resume should have periods, especially after bullet points, but consistency is crucial for professionalism.

  • Bullet Points: Most resume bullet points are concise sentence fragments starting with an action verb (e.g., "Managed a team of 5"). For these fragments, omit the period at the end. This applies to the common query "should resume bullets have periods?".
  • Full Sentences: If you choose to write your bullet points as full sentences (less common, and generally less impactful), then use a period at the end.
  • Consistency is Key: Whichever style you choose for your bullet points, apply it consistently throughout the entire resume. Don't mix and match.
  • Other Sections: Use standard punctuation (including periods) in sections written in prose, like a summary (if written as full sentences) or descriptions.

Recommendation: Using action-verb-led fragments without periods is the most common and often preferred style for bullet points, as it's concise and punchy.

8. File Format Face-Off: Should My Resume Be a PDF or Word Document?

Short Answer: PDF is strongly preferred. When deciding if your resume should be PDF or Word, always save and send it as a PDF unless the job application explicitly requires a different format (like .docx).

  • Preserves Formatting: PDFs look the same on any device, ensuring your carefully crafted layout, fonts, and spacing remain intact. Word documents can look different depending on the software version or operating system.
  • Universally Accessible: Free PDF readers are available for all major platforms.
  • Prevents Accidental Edits: PDFs are harder to accidentally alter by the recipient.
  • ATS Compatibility: Modern Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) are designed to parse PDFs effectively. While older systems might have preferred Word, PDF is now the safer, more professional standard.

Exception: If an online application form specifically requests a .doc or .docx file, provide that format. Keep both PDF and Word versions saved for flexibility.

9. Colorful Choices: Should My Resume Have Color?

Short Answer: Use it very sparingly, if at all. Black text on a white background is the safest bet. Asking "Should my resume have color?" is common. While a touch of subtle color can potentially make a resume stand out, it comes with risks:

  • ATS Issues: Some ATS might struggle to parse colored text or backgrounds correctly.
  • Readability: Poor color choices can make the resume hard to read.
  • Professionalism: Overuse of color can look unprofessional or distracting. Stick to conservative choices if you use any (e.g., a dark blue for headings).
  • Printing: It might not print well in black and white.

Recommendation: Stick to black text and a white background. Use formatting elements like bolding, italics, capitalization, and strategic white space to create visual appeal and structure rather than relying on color. If you must use color, use one single, dark, professional shade (like navy blue or dark grey) sparingly for things like your name or section headers, and test it with ATS checkers if possible.

10. Top Tier: Should My Resume Have a Summary?

Short Answer: Yes, usually. A well-written summary is highly beneficial for most job seekers. A resume summary (sometimes called a Professional Summary or Profile) is a brief 2-4 sentence paragraph at the top of your resume. Its value makes the question "Should my resume have a summary?" lean heavily towards yes. It acts like an "elevator pitch," highlighting your key qualifications, experience level, and career goals.

  • Benefits: It quickly grabs the recruiter's attention, provides context for your experience, and emphasizes your most relevant skills upfront.
  • Who Needs One: Especially helpful for experienced professionals, career changers, or those with extensive experience to synthesize.
  • Summary vs. Objective: Summaries are generally preferred over outdated "Objective" statements (which focused on what you wanted). A summary focuses on the value you bring to the employer. An objective might be suitable for entry-level candidates or drastic career changers with a very specific target role.

Recommendation: Include a tailored, impactful summary at the top of your resume.

11. First Person Perspective: Should My Resume Summary Be in First Person?

Short Answer: No, use the "implied" first person. While the summary is about you, avoid using pronouns like "I," "me," or "my" when considering if the resume summary should be in first person. Write concise statements starting with strong adjectives or job titles.

  • Instead of: "I am a results-oriented marketing manager with 10 years of experience..."
  • Write: "Results-oriented Marketing Manager with 10 years of experience..."
  • Instead of: "My key skills include..."
  • Write: "Key skills include..."

This is the standard convention, saves space, and sounds more professional on a resume.

12. Departure Details: Should I Put My Reason For Leaving On My Resume?

Short Answer: No. Your resume is a marketing document focused on your skills and achievements, not a detailed work history log. Thinking about whether you should put the reason for leaving on your resume? Here's why not:

  • Negative Space: Reasons for leaving (layoff, termination, better opportunity) take up valuable space and often require context that doesn't fit well on a resume.
  • Focus on Positives: Keep the focus on what you accomplished and what you offer the next employer.
  • Interview Discussion: Be prepared to discuss your reasons for leaving previous roles during the interview, but don't proactively offer this information on the resume.

13. Early Education: Should You Put High School On Your Resume?

Short Answer: Generally no, unless it's your highest level of education or you have minimal work experience. Deciding if you should put high school on your resume depends on your current career stage:

Include If:

  • You are currently in high school or just graduated and have little or no college or work experience.
  • The job specifically requires a high school diploma (and you don't have higher education).
  • You attended a particularly prestigious or specialized high school relevant to the job.

Exclude If:

  • You have attended or graduated from college/university. Your higher education makes your high school information redundant.
  • You have several years of work experience. Focus on your professional achievements and higher education.

Rule of Thumb: Once you have post-secondary education or significant work experience, remove your high school details to save space for more relevant information.

Conclusion: Build Your Best Resume

Navigating resume conventions can be tricky, but by following these guidelines, you can create a clear, professional, and effective document. Remember:

  • Keep it concise and relevant.
  • Focus on achievements, not just duties.
  • Tailor it to each job application.
  • Proofread meticulously!
  • Prioritize clarity and professionalism (PDF format, consistent tense, no photos/lies/references directly on the resume).

Your resume is your first impression – make it count! Good luck with your job search!
