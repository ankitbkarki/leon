VR Meeting Tools You Can Actually Use in 2025 (And How They Work)

May 2, 2025

Let’s be honest—most of us rolled our eyes a little when the term “metaverse meetings” started popping up a couple of years ago. It sounded like corporate buzzword bingo mixed with sci-fi. But fast-forward to 2025, and things look... different. Better, even.

Virtual Reality (VR) meetings aren’t just proof-of-concept demos anymore. They're surprisingly usable. Some are even — dare I say — kind of fun? Not every company needs a headset on every desk, of course. But for remote teams, hybrid setups, global conferences, or even quick brainstorming huddles, VR meeting tools are finally earning their place.

In this article, I’ll walk you through the VR meeting tools that actually work in 2025. Not vaporware. Not hype. Real platforms, used by real teams. I’ll explain what each one does, how it works, and whether it’s worth your time.

Why Use VR for Meetings in 2025?

Let’s get this out of the way: regular video calls aren’t going anywhere. They’re fast, simple, and they (usually) don’t require anyone to strap something to their face.

But VR meetings offer a few things that flat screens simply can’t:

  • Spatial presence — You can “stand” next to someone and point at things, just like in real life.

  • Reduced fatigue — Surprisingly, being in a 3D space can feel more natural than staring at a grid of faces.

  • Better whiteboarding and brainstorming — Think sticky notes, sketches, and moving objects around a table in real time.

  • Immersive collaboration — Teams can review 3D models, data visualizations, or shared environments together.

Of course, it’s not perfect. Not everyone has a headset, and let’s be real: some avatars still look like they're straight out of a PlayStation 2 game. But for teams that are ready to experiment—or have already invested in hardware—the experience is getting smoother, more polished, and honestly… pretty useful.

The Top VR Meeting Tools You Can Actually Use in 2025

Here’s a breakdown of the platforms that are leading the pack this year, based on usability, adoption, and actual use cases.

1. Horizon Workrooms (Meta)

  • Best for: Remote teams already using Meta Quest headsets

  • Hardware needed: Meta Quest 2, 3, or Pro

  • Platform support: VR + desktop + mobile

Horizon Workrooms has come a long way since its rocky beta days. In 2025, it’s one of the smoothest, most integrated VR meeting platforms around—especially if your team already uses Meta products. There’s seamless integration with calendar tools, document collaboration, and even passthrough mode, which lets you see your real keyboard and desk while still in VR.

Features that work well:

  • Shared whiteboards with persistent content

  • Real-time spatial audio (yes, it helps you focus)

  • Remote desktop streaming inside VR

  • Hand tracking and facial expression mirroring (with the Quest Pro)

A small downside? You’re locked into the Meta ecosystem. If your team is using other headsets, you’re out of luck.

2. Spatial

  • Best for: Creative teams, designers, and 3D collaboration

  • Hardware needed: Any VR headset, or just a browser

  • Platform support: VR, desktop, mobile

Spatial is like the Canva of VR meetings. It’s visual, intuitive, and accessible even without a headset. You can walk through architectural designs, brainstorm with sticky notes on virtual walls, or just hang out in stylized meeting rooms that look like minimalist art galleries.

In 2025, it’s especially popular with:

  • Design studios

  • Architecture firms

  • Marketing and content teams

Bonus: You can customize avatars using a selfie (they're surprisingly lifelike now), and import 3D assets in minutes. It feels less like a "meeting app" and more like a creative sandbox.

3. MeetinVR

  • Best for: Formal business meetings and consulting firms

  • Hardware needed: Most major VR headsets

  • Platform support: VR only

MeetinVR has positioned itself as the polished, professional alternative. Everything from the UI to the meeting rooms feels more buttoned-up. If you’re trying to pitch a big idea to a client—or run a strategy workshop—it creates a sense of seriousness that some other tools lack.

Key features:

  • Virtual notepads you can export after meetings

  • Room layouts for different purposes (presentations, brainstorming, 1-on-1s)

  • Eye contact simulation (yes, really)

That said, it’s not the most casual option. You won’t find floating donuts or NFT galleries here—and that’s probably for the best.

4. Arthur

  • Best for: Enterprise collaboration at scale

  • Hardware needed: Quest Pro or equivalent

  • Platform support: VR only

Arthur isn’t built for casual users—it’s designed for enterprises with serious collaboration needs. Think management consulting, virtual training, or cross-border project planning. It handles large meeting rooms with dozens of participants, supports 3D object manipulation, and boasts military-grade encryption.

If you’ve got:

  • A large distributed team

  • Sensitive IP to discuss

  • An IT budget that allows premium software

…then Arthur might be the right call. It’s not flashy, but it’s secure, stable, and professional.

5. Rumii

  • Best for: Education and virtual classrooms

  • Hardware needed: PC VR, Oculus, HTC Vive

  • Platform support: VR + desktop

Rumii was originally designed for education, and it still shines in that space. Universities, language schools, and corporate training programs use it to host seminars, teach classes, and run group discussions. In 2025, it’s especially popular with VR-first schools and organizations that want to ditch Zoom for good.

A few standouts:

  • Screen sharing and whiteboard tools

  • Note-taking built into the app

  • Support for recorded lectures and playback

It’s a little clunky compared to newer entrants, but it’s reliable. Think of it as the Google Meet of VR classrooms—not exciting, but dependable.

6. Mozilla Hubs

  • Best for: Lightweight, quick meetups or pop-up events

  • Hardware needed: None (VR optional)

  • Platform support: Browser-based

Here’s a surprise: one of the most accessible VR meeting tools doesn’t even require a headset. Mozilla Hubs is a browser-based 3D space where you can drop in, walk around, and chat with others in real-time. It’s great for community meetups, internal demos, or casual coffee chats.

No downloads. No app store. Just send a link.

What makes it work:

  • Runs on almost any device

  • Easy to set up your own custom “space”

  • Totally free and open-source

It’s not built for Fortune 500 boardrooms, but for quick, spontaneous collaboration, Hubs punches well above its weight.

How VR Meetings Actually Work (A Quick Primer)

If you're new to this, you might be wondering: What happens when you put on the headset? Here’s a simplified walkthrough of how VR meetings typically go:

  1. You launch the app — Usually from inside your VR headset. Some platforms let you join from a desktop, but the full experience is in 3D.

  2. You enter a virtual room — This could look like an office, a lounge, a mountaintop cabin (seriously), or a futuristic spaceship. Teams can choose themes or build custom spaces.

  3. You appear as an avatar — Most tools let you customize it. Some use AI-generated faces, others use cartoonish styles. In 2025, the facial tracking is pretty decent, especially on headsets like the Quest Pro.

  4. You talk, move, interact — Spatial audio lets you hear someone’s voice based on where they’re standing. You can gesture with your hands, write on whiteboards, share documents, or walk across the room to chat privately.

  5. You leave and review — After the meeting, you can often download whiteboards, notes, or recordings. Some tools even offer AI summaries (yeah, that’s becoming a thing).

The Downsides (Let’s Be Honest)

Not everything is rosy in VR land. A few things still kind of suck:

  • Headset fatigue — Wearing a device for more than an hour gets uncomfortable.

  • Motion sickness — Some people still struggle with this, especially in fast-moving environments.

  • Hardware cost — A decent VR headset starts around $300–$500, and you’ll want higher-end gear for better experiences.

  • Learning curve — The first 10 minutes can feel awkward. Like, “where’s the button to sit down?” awkward.

  • Avatar weirdness — No matter how good the tech gets, there’s still a slight uncanny valley vibe to digital avatars.

That said, adoption is growing steadily. According to Statista, VR headset shipments are expected to hit over 20 million units in 2025, up from 13 million in 2023. It’s not mass-market yet, but it’s creeping into mainstream workflows.

When Should You Actually Use VR for Meetings?

Here’s a simple rule of thumb: Use VR when the spatial experience adds value.

✅ Yes, consider VR for:

  • Remote product demos

  • Design collaboration and prototyping

  • Training simulations

  • Virtual offsites or team-building

  • Client presentations in 3D

❌ Probably skip it for:

  • Quick check-ins

  • Text-heavy agendas

  • Participants with limited tech access

  • Folks who don’t want to put on a headset (it’s a real blocker)

Ultimately, it's a tool—not a replacement for everything. Just like we don’t replace every email with a phone call, we shouldn’t replace every video call with a VR meeting.

Final Thoughts

VR meeting tools aren’t just a tech demo anymore. They’re maturing. Slowly, maybe awkwardly, but genuinely. In 2025, if you’ve got the right use case—and the right team—it’s worth trying at least one of these platforms.

You might not love every moment. You might forget to charge your headset (been there). But once you whiteboard an idea in 3D with your teammates scattered across the world, it does something weirdly powerful.

It makes distance feel… less distant.
