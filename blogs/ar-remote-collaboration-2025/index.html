AR for Remote Collaboration: How Teams Are Using Augmented Reality in 2025

May 2, 2025

For most of us, remote work no longer feels temporary. It’s not just a “pandemic thing” anymore—it’s the new normal, or, well, close enough. But here’s the strange part: while video calls and Slack messages are fine (mostly), they’re not exactly built for deep collaboration. You know, the kind where you’re sketching something out on a whiteboard, pointing at a prototype, or walking through a design in real space.

This is where augmented reality (AR) is starting to change the game. And in 2025, it’s no longer just some niche tech for gamers or tech conferences—it’s becoming a surprisingly practical tool for remote teams in all kinds of industries.

We’re not saying every remote worker is walking around in AR glasses (yet), but a growing number of professionals—engineers, designers, field service technicians, even marketers—are now using AR to bridge that "presence gap" and make remote collaboration feel more like the real thing.

Let’s unpack how that’s happening.

1. The Basics: What Does “AR for Remote Collaboration” Actually Mean?

Before diving into use cases, let’s clarify the term. AR (Augmented Reality) overlays digital information—objects, labels, instructions, whatever—on top of your view of the real world, usually through a phone, tablet, or headset.

In the context of remote collaboration, AR allows two or more people in different locations to interact with the same digital content in real time, while still being aware of their physical environment.

So instead of just screen sharing or sending screenshots, you’re both inside the shared work. You might be:

  • Co-designing a 3D model and placing it on your coffee table

  • Giving live visual instructions over someone’s camera feed

  • Walking through a virtual tour of a product as if you’re both standing in the same room

It sounds futuristic. But in 2025? It’s getting oddly normal.

2. Why AR Works Better Than Video for Certain Tasks

Video calls are fine for talking. Sometimes even for brainstorming.

But the second you need to physically point to something, move around, or do something more spatial than verbal—video starts to fall apart. You’re stuck saying things like, “No, the other corner. No, below that one,” and hoping the other person magically understands.

AR helps with this in three big ways:

  1. Spatial context
    You can place 3D models or annotations into a real-world space, so everyone sees what you mean—visually and spatially.

  2. Real-time guidance
    Whether it's technical support or design critique, AR lets one person guide another using arrows, highlights, or even hand gestures overlaid on live video.

  3. Fewer misunderstandings
    The clarity that comes with “seeing” versus “describing” cuts down on back-and-forth.

Honestly, there are moments where it feels like AR adds a shared sixth sense to remote teamwork. It’s not perfect, but it’s impressive.

3. Who’s Actually Using AR for Collaboration in 2025?

Let’s go industry by industry. The applications are surprisingly broad—some very polished, others still kind of clunky—but the momentum is real.

a. Architecture & Design

Designers working in AR can now walk clients through a 3D mockup of a building while standing in an empty lot. They can tweak angles, colors, or even move walls around in real time. Apps like The Wild and Arkio are becoming standard tools for architecture firms.

“It’s like inviting someone into a model home that doesn’t exist yet,” one architect told us. “Clients get it so much faster.”

b. Field Service & Maintenance

Technicians in oil, gas, or manufacturing environments can get remote help from experts without flying them in. A senior engineer can “see” what a junior tech sees through smart glasses and guide them with live AR annotations.

RealWear and Microsoft HoloLens 2 continue to dominate this space. And more companies are experimenting with Apple Vision Pro integration, especially since the 2025 SDK updates improved hand tracking and battery life.

c. Healthcare and Surgery

Yes, it’s happening here too. AR is being used for remote surgical training, diagnostics, and even assisted procedures. A surgeon in New York can virtually guide a team in Nairobi with 3D overlays on live patient scans.

No, it’s not replacing hands-on training, but it’s speeding up education and expanding access where it's desperately needed.

d. Manufacturing & Product Development

3D prototyping has gone remote. Teams working on physical products can now review early-stage models in AR, adjust features together, and even test ergonomics (roughly, at least).

This has cut iteration cycles dramatically—especially for startups that can’t afford to ship physical prototypes around the world five times.

e. Marketing & Sales

Showrooms? Optional. Many companies now use AR experiences to let customers “try” products in their space—everything from furniture to cosmetics.

But on the B2B side, teams are using AR to collaborate on pitch decks, demo tools, and visual walkthroughs that feel far more immersive than screen shares.

5. Challenges (Because of Course There Are)

Okay, let’s pump the brakes for a second. AR isn’t a magic bullet, and it doesn’t always work.

Here’s where things still get tricky:

  • Hardware cost: A full AR collaboration setup can run into the thousands per user. Not every team can justify that.

  • Training: Learning curves are real. Many professionals are still hesitant to adopt “weird” new tools.

  • Battery life: Headsets still struggle with long sessions. It’s not great when your goggles die halfway through a meeting.

  • Discomfort: Let’s be honest—wearing something on your face for hours? Not ideal.

  • Software compatibility: Not every platform plays nicely with others. Integrating AR into existing workflows still takes effort.

That said, companies seem more willing in 2025 to invest in figuring it out. There’s a sense that this won’t be a fad like 3D TVs or Google Glass. The tech finally has a job to do—and it’s doing it well enough to keep interest alive.

6. What's Next? (And What Might Fizzle Out)

There’s a bit of buzz fatigue, sure. Every few years someone says, “This is the year AR goes mainstream.” And then it doesn’t, quite.

But in 2025, something does feel different. Maybe because the remote work problem is finally big and permanent enough to justify these tools. Or maybe it's just because the hardware finally doesn’t suck.

A few trends to watch:

  • More lightweight AR glasses — rumors suggest Apple and Meta will launch slimmer models soon. Less headset, more smart glasses.

  • AI + AR integration — expect assistants that not only share your view but understand what you’re doing and offer suggestions.

  • WebAR collaboration tools — no apps, no installs, just open a link and get to work. This will be huge for accessibility.

And of course, some ideas probably won’t stick. Not every team needs 3D avatars in virtual boardrooms. Sometimes, a simple shared model with voice chat is enough.

Final Thoughts: Will Everyone Be Using AR to Work Together?

No. Not everyone. But more than you’d expect.

In 2025, AR isn’t replacing Zoom or Slack or Figma—it’s supplementing them. It’s carving out a niche for the kind of collaboration that just doesn’t translate well to 2D.

If your work involves space, objects, motion, or environments—AR can give you a new way to be present with your team, even when you’re continents apart.

And for a lot of teams, that’s more than worth the adjustment.

FAQ: Quick Answers to Common Questions

Q: Do I need a headset to use AR for collaboration?
Not necessarily. Many tools work with smartphones and tablets. Headsets just offer a more immersive experience.

Q: Is AR better than VR for remote teamwork?
For most work-related tasks, yes. AR lets you stay aware of your real environment while interacting with virtual content. That’s key for practical use.

Q: What are the biggest downsides of using AR in 2025?
Cost, battery life, and comfort are still barriers. Also, the tech can be buggy depending on your setup.

Q: Can small teams use AR effectively, or is it only for big companies?
Small teams can use AR—especially now that mobile-based AR tools are improving. But headsets remain a big investment.
