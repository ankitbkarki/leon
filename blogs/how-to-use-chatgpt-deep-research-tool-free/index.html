How to Use ChatGPT’s Deep Research Tool for Free

April 30, 2025

There’s a certain thrill in finally finding a tool that just gets what you’re looking for. No awkward keyword tweaking, no ten-tab rabbit holes, no skimming 2,000-word posts that never quite answer your question. Just straight-up, solid research.

That’s what ChatGPT’s Deep Research Tool promises. And if you’re reading this, you probably want to know: can I actually use it for free?

Short answer: yes.
Longer answer: yes — but there are a few caveats, some subtle limitations, and a bit of strategy involved.

Let’s get into it.

First Things First: What Exactly Is the Deep Research Tool?

So, let’s clear this up. The “Deep Research Tool” isn’t a separate app you download. It’s not a plugin or Chrome extension. It’s not even officially branded as a standalone feature (at least, not right now). It’s a capability built into ChatGPT — specifically, into the GPT-4-turbo model, which powers the paid ChatGPT Plus subscription.

But here’s the part people sometimes miss: while it’s mostly associated with the premium plan, there are ways to access similar capabilities without paying — if you know how to nudge the system.

The tool itself is basically a smarter, context-aware way to do multi-step research. Think of it like having a research assistant that can summarize articles, cross-reference sources, detect bias (well, sort of), and even cite material — all in one go. It doesn’t just grab snippets from Wikipedia and call it a day. It actually tries to think through the logic, often in surprisingly thoughtful ways.

You can ask it to:

  • Compare studies or viewpoints

  • Pull stats across multiple sources

  • Summarize complex academic papers

  • Generate bibliographies or citations

  • Dig deeper on ambiguous or controversial topics

It’s not perfect, of course. It occasionally invents sources (more on that later), and sometimes gives answers that feel a little too confident. But as research tools go, it’s... well, pretty incredible.

So... How Do You Use It Without Paying?

Let’s be honest. Most people asking how to use this for free aren’t just curious — they’re trying to dodge the $20/month ChatGPT Plus subscription. Fair enough. Twenty bucks isn’t nothing.

There are a few workarounds, and while none are perfect, they’re surprisingly useful if you’re a bit patient and willing to get creative.

1. Use Microsoft Copilot (Free Version)

Wait — Microsoft? What does that have to do with ChatGPT?

Actually, a lot.

Microsoft has integrated GPT-4 (yes, the full thing) into its Bing-powered “Copilot” search experience. And the kicker? It’s free.

You can access it in a few ways:

  • Through the Copilot website (copilot.microsoft.com)

  • In the Edge browser sidebar

  • In some versions of Microsoft Word or Excel (though these may push you toward a Microsoft 365 subscription)

The trick is to use Copilot’s “Creative” or “Precise” mode, depending on your goal. It can handle fairly deep research queries and often pulls up real-time sources (something ChatGPT, by itself, doesn’t always do unless connected to the web).

Sure, the tone’s a bit different. And you can’t customize the personality like you can in ChatGPT. But under the hood? It’s powered by the same GPT-4-turbo architecture.

2. Use ChatGPT Free Plan + Smart Prompting

This one’s a bit more nuanced.

Technically, the free ChatGPT tier only gives you GPT-3.5. So you might assume you’re out of luck. But here’s the thing: if your research isn’t too complex — or if you're guiding it well — GPT-3.5 can still mimic “deep” research behaviors with the right prompts.

For example, instead of just saying:

"Summarize the effects of climate change on agriculture."

Try something like:

“Imagine you’re writing a research paper on how climate change affects crop yields in developing countries. What would your outline look like? What sources would you cite? Break it down by region.”

This kind of role-playing or scenario-based prompting pushes the model to behave more like a research assistant rather than a chatbot tossing facts at you. It may not reference real papers unless you supply them — but you’d be surprised how far it can go with context.

3. Use Open Access Academic Tools in Tandem

This one’s more of a combo move.

Let’s say you find an article on Google Scholar or PubMed. You copy-paste that article — or a chunk of it — into ChatGPT and ask:

“Summarize this in 5 bullet points. Then tell me the main argument, any assumptions the author makes, and whether the evidence seems strong.”

Even GPT-3.5 can handle this decently.

If you do happen to be using GPT-4, the results get even sharper. It will often draw inferences, spot vague reasoning, or reframe the argument more clearly than the original author did. (Which is a bit unnerving, actually.)

So while ChatGPT can’t browse the web on the free tier, you can still feed it raw data and ask for help interpreting it — which, let’s be honest, is most of what “deep research” really is.

What If You Want the Actual Deep Research Capabilities?

Okay, so say you’ve tried the free options, and they’re good but not quite “wow” enough. If you're looking for something more powerful — real-time web access, PDF uploads, memory, custom instructions — that’s where the paid GPT-4 tier comes in.

Here’s what that includes:

  • File Uploads: You can drop in long PDFs, spreadsheets, or research papers and ask it to analyze, summarize, compare, etc.

  • Web Browsing: It can look up fresh information (though it sometimes still needs a nudge to cite properly)

  • Memory: It remembers past conversations, your preferences, and what you’ve asked before (if enabled)

  • Custom GPTs: You can create your own “researcher” GPT that acts exactly the way you want

It’s not free, obviously. But if you’re doing consistent research — say, you're a student, freelancer, analyst, or just very curious — the cost might actually save you time and, well, brainpower.

Can You Get a Free Trial of GPT-4?

As of now, OpenAI doesn’t offer a formal free trial of GPT-4-turbo through ChatGPT itself. That said, there are some ways to test the waters:

1. Look for Free Credits via Third Parties

Some platforms that integrate ChatGPT (like educational tools or productivity apps) will let you use GPT-4 as part of a free trial or limited tier. It’s hit-or-miss, and not always advertised clearly, but worth a quick search.

2. Use GPT-4 for Free in Developer Mode (Sort Of)

If you’re comfortable with APIs and willing to register for OpenAI’s platform (platform.openai.com), you can get $5 of free credit when you sign up. That lets you run GPT-4 queries via the API.

It’s not the most user-friendly approach, and it’s time-limited — but it does give you a taste of what GPT-4 can do behind the scenes.

Tips to Make the Most of Free ChatGPT Research

Whether you're using GPT-3.5, Copilot, or some hybrid setup, here are a few ways to stretch its usefulness:

Use Multi-Step Prompts

Don’t just ask a question and move on. Dig deeper. For example:

  1. Ask for a basic summary.

  2. Then ask for pros/cons.

  3. Then ask how the idea evolved over time.

  4. Finally, ask how experts disagree.

This builds layers into the research — and feels closer to how you'd actually think through a topic.

Paste in Source Material

If the AI can’t browse, bring the data to it. Whether it’s a quote, excerpt, or full article, giving it material to work with dramatically improves the quality of output.

Ask It to Challenge Itself

This is an odd trick — but a good one. After you get an answer, say:

“Now argue against this conclusion.”

It forces the model to think more critically, spot gaps, and present a more balanced view — even when it’s just you talking to a machine.

What Are the Limitations?

Let’s not sugarcoat it. Even in GPT-4, “deep research” has its limits.

  • Made-Up Sources: Sometimes it hallucinates citations. Always verify.

  • Shallow Analysis: If your prompt is vague, you’ll get a Wikipedia-level answer.

  • No Real-Time Judgment: It doesn’t “know” whether something is credible unless you specify.

  • Bias: Like all models, it reflects the biases in its training data — and those biases do show up, especially on complex or polarized topics.

And then there’s just the reality that, despite the name, AI doesn’t “understand” research the way a person does. It can’t go down a rabbit hole out of curiosity or change course mid-project because it noticed something odd. (At least not yet.)

Final Thoughts

Here’s the thing: for most casual researchers — students, writers, independent thinkers, and even some professionals — the free versions of ChatGPT or GPT-powered tools like Microsoft Copilot can get you surprisingly far.

You won’t get every bell and whistle, sure. But with smart prompts and a little patience, you can absolutely simulate the deep research experience. Maybe not flawlessly, but realistically. And isn’t that kind of the point?

You don’t always need perfect. You just need useful. And on that front, ChatGPT — free or not — is already changing the game.
